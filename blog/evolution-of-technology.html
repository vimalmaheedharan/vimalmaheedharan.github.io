A short history, told as one line

From clay tablets to thinking machines

Every technology inherits the one before it. Follow the thread — mechanical, electrical, digital, intelligent — as it runs through eleven inventions that rewired how humans record, connect, and think.

Scroll the timeline
I — Mechanical Era
c. 2700 BCE

The Abacus

Beads on rods turn arithmetic into something you can touch. The first tool built purely to extend human calculation.

1440

The Printing Press

Gutenberg's movable type turns copying a book from a year of labor into an afternoon — the first machine to mass-produce ideas.

II — Electrical Era
1837

The Telegraph

Morse's dots and dashes send words down a wire at the speed of electricity, shrinking a continent to the space of a message.

1876

The Telephone

Bell converts voice itself into signal. For the first time, distance stops mattering to conversation.

1946

ENIAC

Thirty tons of vacuum tubes become the first general-purpose electronic computer — arithmetic finally has no hands left in it.

III — Digital Era
1969

ARPANET

Four university computers exchange the first packet-switched message. The network that becomes the internet takes its first breath.

1975

The Personal Computer

The Altair 8800 puts a computer on a desk instead of in a basement — and hands ordinary people the machine that once filled a room.

1991

The World Wide Web

Tim Berners-Lee links documents across machines with a single click. The network becomes a place, not just a wire.

2007

The Smartphone

The iPhone folds the phone, the computer, and the camera into a single pane of glass that fits in a pocket.

IV — Intelligent Era
2012

Deep Learning Takes Off

A neural network named AlexNet crushes an image-recognition contest, proving machines can learn to see rather than being told how.

2022

Conversational AI

Large language models move from research paper to everyday tool — machines that don't just compute, but converse.

Each layer becomes the floor for the next.

The abacus made arithmetic portable. The press made ideas copyable. The wire made distance irrelevant. The chip made computation personal. The network made knowledge shared. And now, the model makes language itself a tool. The thread never actually breaks — it just keeps getting faster.

End of timeline
